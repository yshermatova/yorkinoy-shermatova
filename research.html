Overview

Research

My research bridges applied mathematics and ecology, using mathematical models to understand how diseases propagate through forest ecosystems, how biodiversity modulates epidemic risk, and how Earth-observation data can serve as an early-warning system for invasive forest pathogens.

Recent Project (Postdoctoral)

Detecting Invasive Tree Diseases via Spaceborne Phenology

During my postdoctoral appointment at the University of Minnesota (Jan 2025 – May 2026), I developed methods to detect tree diseases — especially oak wilt — using satellite-derived phenological signals and machine learning. Invasive forest pathogens can alter leaf-out timing, canopy density, and seasonal color change in detectable ways before mortality becomes visible to ground crews.

By coupling remote sensing imagery with agent-based epidemiological models, the project produced landscape-scale disease risk maps guiding management interventions in eastern North America's oak forests.

PI: Prof. Jesús Pinto-Ledezma · Total award: $435,937 · Funded by MITPPC

Doctoral Research

Oak Wilt Disease & Biodiversity

My PhD thesis developed mathematical models to explore how oak wilt spreads through forests and how species diversity — particularly the presence of white oaks — shapes epidemic outcomes.

White Oak as a Buffer Species

Mathematical analysis showing that white oaks — which are resistant to oak wilt — can slow or halt epidemic spread in mixed forests by disrupting root-to-root transmission pathways.

Agent-Based Spread Models

Individual-based simulations capturing tree-by-tree oak wilt spread, accounting for spatial heterogeneity, canopy structure, and the role of insect vectors vs. root grafts.

Disease–Fire Interactions

Dynamical systems analysis of feedbacks between oak wilt mortality, fuel accumulation, and wildfire risk — exploring coupled disturbance regimes in Midwestern savannas.
