Focus Areas

Research Interests

My work sits at the intersection of applied mathematics and ecology — using mathematical models to understand how diseases spread through forests, how biodiversity shapes ecosystem resilience, and how satellite observations can detect emerging threats.

Disease Dynamics in Forest Ecosystems

Mathematical modeling of oak wilt disease spread — understanding how species diversity, landscape structure, and root connections govern epidemic trajectories in mixed forests.

Individual & Agent-Based Models

Simulating tree-level interactions, species coexistence, and disease-fire feedbacks using agent-based frameworks to capture ecological heterogeneity that aggregate models miss.

Remote Sensing & Machine Learning

Integrating spaceborne phenological observations with ecological models to detect invasive tree diseases at landscape scale — bridging satellite data and field ecology.

Dynamical Systems & Climate Mathematics

Applying ODE/PDE frameworks and dynamical systems theory to ecological and climate problems — including polar vortex dynamics, COVID-19 case fatality modeling, and biodiversity theory.

Theoretical Ecology & Biodiversity

Exploring how species coexistence mechanisms — competitive exclusion, facilitation, trait diversity — shape community assembly and disease resilience in temperate forest systems.

Computational Methods

Python and MATLAB for large-scale simulations; statistical analysis of ecological data; developing reusable computational tools for mathematical ecology research.

Current Work

Detecting Invasive Tree Diseases from Space

As a postdoctoral associate in the Pinto-Ledezma Lab, I am developing methods to detect and monitor invasive forest pathogens — including oak wilt — using spaceborne phenological signals and machine learning.

This work is funded by the Minnesota Invasive Terrestrial Plants and Pests Center (MITPPC) with a total award of $435,937, and combines remote sensing, ecological modeling, and epidemiological theory.
